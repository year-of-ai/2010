# Lionel Messi's 2010 Ballon d'Or

**Category**: People  
**Key figures**: Lionel Messi, Andrés Iniesta, Xavi Hernández, Cristiano Ronaldo (Real Madrid), Pep Guardiola, FC Barcelona

## Summary

In January 2011, Lionel Messi was awarded the inaugural FIFA Ballon d'Or for his performance during the 2010 calendar year — the first edition of this merged award, which combined the historic France Football Ballon d'Or (dating to 1956) and the FIFA World Player of the Year prize into a single, globally elected accolade. At 23, Messi claimed the award for a dominant 2010 campaign in which he led FC Barcelona to the La Liga title and the Copa del Rey under manager Pep Guardiola. The three-man shortlist — Messi, Andrés Iniesta, and Xavi Hernández — was an all-Barcelona lineup, a near-unprecedented grouping that reflected the Catalan club's collective hegemony over world football at the time. Messi garnered the largest vote share, his margin reflecting both statistical output and the consensus that his intricate dribbling, spatial intelligence, and goal-scoring had elevated Barcelona's already extraordinary team.

Messi had won the standalone France Football Ballon d'Or in December 2009, making the 2010 FIFA Ballon d'Or his second consecutive individual honor of comparable prestige. The award marked a pivotal moment in a generational rivalry: Cristiano Ronaldo — who had joined Real Madrid from Manchester United in summer 2009 for a then-world-record transfer fee of £80 million (approximately €94 million) — finished outside the three-man shortlist entirely, behind both Iniesta and Xavi, signaling that Barcelona's collective brilliance was overshadowing Madrid's individual star power in the eyes of the global electorate.

## The 2009–10 Barcelona Season

Barcelona's 2009–10 La Liga campaign was among the most commanding in modern Spanish football. The club finished with 99 points — winning the title by three points over Real Madrid — while averaging more than 60 percent possession per match. Messi finished the season as La Liga's top scorer with 34 league goals, the highest single-season total by a Barcelona player in the modern era at that point. He contributed across all competitions to a team that scored more than 150 goals across La Liga and cup competitions combined.

The Copa del Rey victory over Sevilla in the final on May 19, 2010, added a domestic double. Barcelona's run to the Champions League semifinal — where they lost to eventual finalists Inter Milan under José Mourinho — remained their ceiling in European competition that year, underscoring that even the era's best club had limits when tactical discipline was maximally applied. Guardiola's 4-3-3 / false-nine hybrid, in which Messi was nominally listed as a center forward but operated between the lines, was widely studied and imitated by coaching staffs across Europe.

## Argentina and the 2010 World Cup

Messi's club dominance in 2010 was shadowed by international disappointment. Argentina, coached by Diego Maradona, progressed through the group stage and round of sixteen before suffering a 4–0 quarterfinal defeat to Germany on July 3 in Cape Town — Argentina's heaviest defeat in World Cup knockout competition in decades. Germany's structured pressing and transitional speed overwhelmed Argentina's attack-first system. Messi, despite creating chances, did not score during the entire tournament.

The Ballon d'Or jury weighed full-calendar-year performance, and Messi's La Liga dominance proved decisive over the World Cup disappointment. The contrast — transcendent club performer, frustrated international — became a recurring theme in assessments of Messi's career through the 2010s, one not resolved until Argentina's Copa América victory in 2021 and FIFA World Cup triumph in 2022.

## The New Award Format and Electorate

The 2010 FIFA Ballon d'Or introduced a significant structural change to individual football recognition. Previously, the France Football Ballon d'Or could only be won by European players or players performing in Europe; the FIFA World Player of the Year had a global electorate of national team coaches and captains. The merged award retained the global electorate — coaches and captains of all FIFA-affiliated national teams — expanding the voter base from a few dozen European journalists to over 500 individual voters worldwide. This change made non-European perspectives structurally influential for the first time, though the award's results in subsequent years remained dominated by European-based players.

The ceremony for the inaugural FIFA Ballon d'Or was held on January 10, 2011, at the Kongresshaus in Zurich, Switzerland — a venue also used for FIFA's broader Congress functions. The presence of all three finalists (Messi, Iniesta, Xavi) as Barcelona teammates added an unusual symmetry: no single club had previously occupied the entire shortlist.

## The Messi–Ronaldo Rivalry: Context in 2010

The rivalry between Messi and Ronaldo had become football's defining personal contest by 2010. Ronaldo had won the 2008 France Football Ballon d'Or and the UEFA Champions League with Manchester United before his high-profile transfer to Real Madrid in summer 2009. In his first Real Madrid season (2009–10), Ronaldo scored 33 La Liga goals — an elite total — yet could not prevent Barcelona from winning the title. His exclusion from the 2010 Ballon d'Or shortlist, in favor of two of his direct rivals at Barcelona (Iniesta and Xavi), was itself a statement about how the electorate valued collective excellence.

The rivalry would intensify throughout the decade: Ronaldo won the FIFA Ballon d'Or in 2013 and 2014, while Messi added 2011, 2012, 2015, 2019, 2021, and 2023. Each award cycle reignited a debate about the relative merits of Messi's positional creativity versus Ronaldo's athletic power and finishing — a debate that structured football media through the 2010s.

## Significance

Messi's inaugural FIFA Ballon d'Or crystallized Barcelona's dominant era under Guardiola and validated tiki-taka as the sport's leading tactical philosophy. The all-Barcelona shortlist was a signal: a single club's academy system (La Masia, which had developed all three finalists) had produced the three best players in world football simultaneously — an argument for positional coaching, technical skill development from youth level, and possession-based system football that influenced club academies worldwide. At a cultural level, the award represented a validation of the Argentine footballing tradition (*la nuestra* — "our [football]"), rooted in technical mastery and creative improvisation. Messi's four consecutive FIFA Ballon d'Ors (2010–2013, counting from the merged award's first year) established a benchmark for sustained individual dominance unprecedented in the award's history at that point.
