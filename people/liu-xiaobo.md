# Liu Xiaobo — Nobel Peace Prize Winner

**Category**: People  
**Key figures**: Liu Xiaobo (laureate), Liu Xia (wife, also detained under house arrest without charges), Thorbjørn Jagland (Nobel Committee Chairman), Václav Havel (Czech playwright and former president, supporter), Hu Jintao (Chinese President), Yang Jiechi (Chinese Foreign Minister)

## Summary

Liu Xiaobo, a Chinese literary critic, professor, and human rights activist, was awarded the 2010 Nobel Peace Prize on October 8 "for his long and non-violent struggle for fundamental human rights in China." At the time of the award, Liu was imprisoned in Jinzhou Prison, Liaoning Province, serving an eleven-year sentence for subversion, imposed following his role in drafting Charter 08 in December 2008. He became the first ethnic Chinese citizen to receive a Nobel Prize while residing in China, and the first Nobel Peace laureate since Carl von Ossietzky in 1935 to be imprisoned in his own country at the time of the award.

The Norwegian Nobel Committee's decision sparked immediate diplomatic tensions and censorship in China. Beijing blocked international news coverage of the announcement, summoned the Norwegian ambassador to lodge an official protest, and put pressure on other nations not to attend the December 10 Oslo ceremony. At the ceremony, Liu's chair sat empty—he was unable to attend, and China had detained his wife Liu Xia under extrajudicial house arrest to prevent her from accepting on his behalf.

Liu's path to the prize reflected decades of activism spanning the 1989 Tiananmen Square protests, multiple imprisonments for pro-democracy advocacy, and his leadership of China's intellectual resistance to authoritarianism.

## Early Life and Academic Career

Liu Xiaobo was born December 28, 1955, in Changchun, Jilin Province, into a family of intellectuals. He grew up during the Cultural Revolution (1966–1976) and witnessed its suppression of intellectual and cultural life firsthand—an experience that shaped his later commitment to freedom of expression. He graduated from Jilin University in 1982 with a degree in literature and earned a doctorate in literary theory from Beijing Normal University in 1988, writing his dissertation on aesthetics and freedom.

By the late 1980s, Liu had emerged as a prominent and provocative literary critic, known for challenging the prevailing norms of Chinese intellectual culture. His 1986 essay "Crisis! New Period Literature Faces a Crisis" attacked contemporary Chinese literature as derivative and sycophantic, generating controversy and establishing his reputation as an enfant terrible of Chinese letters. He spent time at Columbia University, Oslo University, and the University of Hawaii as a visiting scholar during 1988–1989.

## Tiananmen Square (1989) and First Imprisonment

When the Tiananmen Square protests erupted in April 1989, Liu Xiaobo cut short his overseas appointments and returned to China to join the demonstrators. He became one of the most prominent intellectual voices of the protest movement and, on June 2, launched a hunger strike alongside Taiwanese singer-songwriter Hou Dejian, sociologist Zhou Duo, and journalist Gao Xin—the four who became known as the "Four Gentlemen of Tiananmen." Their hunger strike was intended to galvanize the flagging protest movement just days before the June 4 crackdown.

On the night of June 3–4, as tanks moved toward Tiananmen Square, Liu negotiated a deal with PLA commanders that allowed the remaining students to evacuate peacefully through a safe corridor—an intervention widely credited with preventing an even larger massacre. His role was later acknowledged by Chinese government officials as having reduced casualties.

Despite this, Liu was arrested on June 4, 1989. He was detained for 20 months, released in 1991 without formal charges, but stripped of his teaching position and banned from publishing. He was imprisoned again in 1995 for organizing a petition campaign calling for democratic reforms and spent time in a reeducation-through-labor camp from 1996 to 1999.

## Charter 08

Liu Xiaobo's most consequential act came in 2008, when he co-authored and helped organize the distribution of **Charter 08**, a manifesto modeled in part on Czechoslovakia's Charter 77. The document called for:

- Constitutional democracy with separation of powers
- An independent judiciary
- Protection of human rights, including freedom of speech, press, assembly, and religion
- Multiparty elections and direct elections for senior officials
- Abolition of reeducation-through-labor camps
- Economic liberalization with protection of private property

Charter 08 was released on December 10, 2008—the 60th anniversary of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. More than 300 Chinese academics, lawyers, and intellectuals had signed it before publication; within weeks, signatures from tens of thousands of Chinese citizens had been collected online, despite immediate censorship. Police arrested Liu on the evening of December 8, two days before the document was released.

Liu was tried on December 23, 2009, in a closed hearing that lasted barely two hours. He was convicted of "incitement to subvert state power" and sentenced to eleven years in prison plus two years' deprivation of political rights—one of the harshest sentences ever handed down for dissident activity in China.

## The Nobel Prize and Chinese Response

The Nobel Committee's announcement on October 8, 2010 immediately produced one of the most dramatic responses to a Nobel award since the prize itself began in 1901. Norwegian television was blacked out in China within seconds of the announcement. CNN and BBC World News were blocked; Chinese social media users who tried to search for Liu's name or "Nobel Prize" found their searches returned blank results.

Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesperson Ma Zhaoxu stated that Liu was a "criminal" who had "violated Chinese law" and that the award was "an obscenity to the Nobel Peace Prize." Foreign Minister Yang Jiechi summoned the Norwegian ambassador and warned that the award would damage bilateral relations. In the weeks that followed, Chinese diplomats lobbied dozens of countries not to send representatives to the Oslo ceremony. Ultimately, at least 45 of the 65 invited countries attended the December 10, 2010 ceremony; China and 18 other countries—including Russia, Iran, Cuba, Venezuela, Saudi Arabia, Pakistan, and Egypt—declined to attend.

The Norwegian Nobel Committee invited Liu's wife, Liu Xia, who had been placed under extrajudicial house arrest immediately after the Nobel announcement (despite having never been charged with any crime), to travel to Oslo to accept the prize. China refused to allow her to leave. His son and other family members were also prevented from attending. The prize chair sat empty during the ceremony—a powerful visual symbol that recalled the empty chair of the Nazi-condemned journalist Carl von Ossietzky at the 1935 Nobel ceremony.

Norwegian actress Liv Ullmann read aloud a statement Liu had made to the court at his 2009 trial: *"I have no enemies and no hatred. None of the police who have monitored, arrested, and interrogated me, the prosecutors who indicted me, or the judges who convicted me are my enemies."*

## Later Life and Death

Liu Xiaobo remained imprisoned in Jinzhou Prison until May 2017, when Chinese authorities announced he had been diagnosed with late-stage liver cancer. He was released on medical parole on May 23, 2017, and transferred to a hospital in Shenyang. International medical experts who visited him—including American and German physicians—recommended that he be allowed to travel abroad for treatment. Chinese authorities refused, stating he was too ill to travel.

Liu Xiaobo died on July 13, 2017, at Shenyang Medical University Cancer Hospital, at age 61—the second Nobel Peace laureate to die in state custody. His wife Liu Xia, who had been held under house arrest since 2010 without charge, was finally permitted to leave China for Germany in July 2018, following international pressure.

The Nobel Peace Prize medal and diploma were held by the Norwegian Nobel Institute on Liu's behalf. In 2019, a Swedish company developed a "Liu Xiaobo Award" in his honor; the Nobel Committee does not issue an official award in a laureate's name.

## Significance

Liu Xiaobo's 2010 Nobel Peace Prize represented an extraordinary international endorsement of nonviolent resistance to authoritarian rule. The award sent a powerful message on behalf of human rights globally, granting recognition to a political prisoner in a major world power—a rare diplomatic stance that prioritized principles over diplomatic relations. For international human-rights movements, the award validated Charter 08's vision of democratic reform and galvanized support for Chinese dissidents and activists.

Domestically within China, the government's censorship and hostile response to the award underscored the Communist Party's sensitivity to international pressure and alternative visions of governance. The incident demonstrated both the power and limits of international recognition: while the Nobel Prize elevated Liu's status globally and among diaspora communities, it could not secure his freedom or change Chinese government policy during his lifetime.

Liu's recognition also positioned him as a symbol of the broader Asian pro-democracy movement, resonating with activists in Taiwan, Hong Kong, Myanmar, and Vietnam. His Charter 08 directly influenced the Umbrella Movement's demands in Hong Kong (2014) and was cited by many Hong Kong activists as foundational to their understanding of peaceful civil disobedience against authoritarian rule.

His life and work became touchstones for scholarly and activist discussions of nonviolent resistance and the role of intellectuals in political change—drawing frequent comparisons to figures like Gandhi, Mandela, and Solzhenitsyn.

## Key Facts

- **Birth and Death**: Born December 28, 1955, Changchun, Jilin; died July 13, 2017, Shenyang, aged 61.
- **Education**: BA Jilin University (1982); PhD Beijing Normal University (1988).
- **Tiananmen Role (1989)**: One of the "Four Gentlemen" hunger strikers; negotiated peaceful student withdrawal on June 3–4, credited with preventing additional casualties.
- **Multiple Imprisonments**: 1989–1991 (counterrevolutionary activities); 1995–1996 (democracy petition); 1996–1999 (reeducation-through-labor); 2009–2017 (Charter 08/subversion).
- **Charter 08 (December 2008)**: 19-point pro-democracy manifesto; signed by 300+ at publication, tens of thousands collected within weeks; based in part on Czechoslovakia's Charter 77.
- **Arrest and Sentencing**: Arrested December 8, 2008; convicted December 23, 2009; sentenced to 11 years for "incitement to subvert state power."
- **Nobel Prize (October 8, 2010)**: Awarded by chairman Thorbjørn Jagland; first ethnic Chinese resident of China to win any Nobel Prize; cited "for his long and non-violent struggle for fundamental human rights in China."
- **Ceremony (December 10, 2010)**: Chair left empty; Liv Ullmann read Liu's court statement; Liu Xia prevented from attending; China and 18 other countries declined to attend at China's request.
- **China's Response**: International broadcast blocking; ambassador summoned; 19-country absence (China plus 18 others); Liu Xia placed under extrajudicial house arrest through 2018.
- **Death in Custody**: Liu died July 13, 2017, in state custody — the second Nobel Peace laureate to die under state detention.
- **Liu Xia**: Wife, also subjected to extrajudicial house arrest from 2010–2018 despite never being formally charged with any crime.
- **Influence on Charter 77**: Liu explicitly cited Václav Havel's Charter 77 as a model; Havel supported Liu's Nobel nomination.
