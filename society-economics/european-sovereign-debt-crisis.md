# European Sovereign Debt Crisis and the First Greek Bailout

**Category:** Society & Economics

**Key figures:** George Papandreou (Greek Prime Minister), George Papaconstantinou (Greek Finance Minister), Angela Merkel (German Chancellor), Jean-Claude Trichet (President of the European Central Bank), Dominique Strauss-Kahn (Managing Director of the International Monetary Fund), Olli Rehn (European Commissioner for Economic and Monetary Affairs), Jean-Claude Juncker (President of the Eurogroup)

## Summary

During 2010 the eurozone entered the acute phase of a sovereign debt crisis that would test the foundations of the European single currency for the rest of the decade. The crisis crystallised in Greece, whose newly elected government revealed in late 2009 that the country's budget deficit was far larger than previously reported. Locked out of affordable bond-market financing, Greece on 23 April 2010 became the first euro-area member to request an international rescue, and on 2 May 2010 the Eurogroup and the International Monetary Fund agreed a three-year €110 billion loan programme — the first euro-area bailout — in exchange for severe austerity measures.

The Greek rescue did not contain the panic. Fearing that the crisis would spread to other heavily indebted members, European finance ministers met over the weekend of 9–10 May 2010 and assembled a far larger backstop: the temporary European Financial Stability Facility (EFSF), with a lending capacity of up to €440 billion, combined with up to €60 billion from the European Financial Stabilisation Mechanism and up to €250 billion from the IMF, for a headline safety net of €750 billion. The European Commission, the European Central Bank, and the IMF — collectively dubbed the "Troika" — became the joint overseers of the adjustment programmes. By the end of the year the contagion had reached Ireland, which accepted an €85 billion rescue on 28–29 November 2010.

## Origins: the Greek deficit revelation

After the October 2009 Greek general election brought George Papandreou's PASOK government to power, the new administration disclosed that the 2009 budget deficit would reach roughly 12.7 percent of gross domestic product — roughly double the figure the outgoing government had reported — a number subsequently revised upward to about 15.4 percent. Public debt exceeded 120 percent of GDP. The revelations destroyed investor confidence in the reliability of Greek fiscal statistics and triggered a sustained rise in Greek borrowing costs, as the interest-rate spread between Greek and German government bonds widened sharply.

Credit-rating agencies downgraded Greek sovereign debt repeatedly in early 2010. On 27 April 2010 Standard & Poor's cut Greece's rating to BB+, or "junk" status, the first time a eurozone member had lost its investment-grade rating, effectively closing the bond markets to Athens and forcing it to seek official assistance.

## The first bailout

On 23 April 2010, speaking from the island of Kastellorizo, Prime Minister Papandreou formally requested the activation of an EU–IMF support package. On 2 May 2010 the euro-area member states and the IMF agreed the First Economic Adjustment Programme for Greece: €110 billion over three years, comprising roughly €80 billion in bilateral loans from euro-area governments, pooled and administered by the European Commission, and about €30 billion from the IMF. In return, the Greek parliament adopted a far-reaching austerity package — increases in value-added tax, cuts to public-sector wages and pensions, tax rises, and structural reforms — intended to reduce the deficit below the EU's 3 percent threshold over several years.

## Austerity and social unrest

The austerity conditions provoked widespread anger in Greece. On 5 May 2010 trade unions staged a nationwide general strike, and one of the largest demonstrations in modern Greek history converged on the parliament in Athens. During the protests, assailants threw petrol bombs at a branch of Marfin Egnatia Bank in central Athens; three employees — among them a pregnant woman — died of asphyxiation after being trapped inside. The deaths became one of the defining images of the crisis and underscored the social cost of the adjustment programmes that would recur across the eurozone periphery.

## The €750 billion backstop

The Greek package failed to halt market contagion to other vulnerable members, notably Portugal, Spain, and Ireland. Over the weekend of 9–10 May 2010 the Economic and Financial Affairs Council (Ecofin) agreed an emergency stabilisation mechanism. Its centrepiece was the European Financial Stability Facility, a special-purpose vehicle incorporated in Luxembourg and backed by guarantees from euro-area governments, with a lending capacity of up to €440 billion and an initial three-year mandate. The EFSF could be combined with up to €60 billion from the European Financial Stabilisation Mechanism — funds the European Commission could raise against the EU budget — and up to €250 billion from the IMF, producing a headline figure of €750 billion. In parallel, the European Central Bank launched its Securities Markets Programme to purchase eurozone government bonds on the secondary market, its first major departure from orthodox monetary policy during the crisis.

## Contagion: Ireland

The backstop did not end the crisis. Ireland's banking system, crippled by losses from a collapsed property bubble that the state had guaranteed, drove the country's deficit to extraordinary levels in 2010. On 28–29 November 2010, Ireland became the second euro-area member to seek a rescue, agreeing an €85 billion programme that combined EU and IMF funds with Ireland's own reserves. Portugal would follow in 2011, and Greece would require a second programme in 2012, by which time the EFSF was being superseded by the permanent European Stability Mechanism.

## Significance

The events of 2010 transformed a national fiscal scandal into a structural crisis of the euro. They exposed a fundamental design flaw in the monetary union: member states shared a single currency and monetary policy but retained independent fiscal policies, with no central mechanism to rescue a member that lost market access. The improvised rescues of 2010 — and the institutions created to deliver them — became the template for the EFSF's permanent successor, the European Stability Mechanism, established in 2012, and for a decade of debate over fiscal integration, austerity, and the role of the ECB as a lender of last resort. The crisis also entrenched the "Troika" model of conditional lending and reshaped the politics of southern Europe, where austerity programmes fuelled the rise of new political movements throughout the 2010s.
