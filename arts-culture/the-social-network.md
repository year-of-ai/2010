# The Social Network (2010 film)

**Category:** Arts & Culture
**Key figures:** David Fincher (director), Aaron Sorkin (screenplay), Jesse Eisenberg, Andrew Garfield, Justin Timberlake, Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross (score)

## Summary

*The Social Network* is a 2010 American biographical drama film directed by David Fincher and written by Aaron Sorkin, adapted from Ben Mezrich's 2009 book *The Accidental Billionaires: The Founding of Facebook, a Tale of Sex, Money, Genius and Betrayal*. The film dramatizes the founding of Facebook and the subsequent legal disputes among its co-founders.

Jesse Eisenberg stars as Mark Zuckerberg, with Andrew Garfield as co-founder Eduardo Saverin, Justin Timberlake as Napster co-founder Sean Parker, and Armie Hammer in a dual role as the Winklevoss twins. The film is structured around two parallel legal depositions — one filed by Saverin, who was diluted out of the company, and one by the Winklevoss twins, who alleged that Zuckerberg had stolen their idea — interspersed with dramatizations of the events under dispute.

The film premiered at the New York Film Festival on September 24, 2010, and was released theatrically on October 1, 2010. It was produced on a budget of approximately $40 million and earned $226 million at the worldwide box office, making it a significant commercial success. The score — a brooding, electronic work composed by Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross — was released on September 28, 2010.

Critical reception was overwhelmingly positive. The film was nominated for eight Academy Awards at the 83rd ceremony (held February 2011), winning three: Best Adapted Screenplay (Aaron Sorkin), Best Original Score (Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross), and Best Film Editing. It lost Best Picture to *The King's Speech*. It also won four Golden Globe Awards including Best Motion Picture — Drama and Best Director.

## Production and Screenplay

Aaron Sorkin has said he had never used Facebook when he began adapting Ben Mezrich's book, and that he treated the story not as a technology narrative but as a story about friendship and betrayal. His screenplay employs a classical Greek drama structure — hubris, betrayal, nemesis — compressed into a non-linear frame in which the depositions function as a Greek chorus, interrogating the action from a temporal distance. Sorkin conducted multiple interviews with Eduardo Saverin during the research process; Mark Zuckerberg declined to participate. The screenplay underwent extensive rewrites in collaboration with Fincher, who pushed Sorkin to deepen the emotional specificity of the Zuckerberg-Saverin relationship.

David Fincher, known for precision filmmaking on films such as *Fight Club* (1999) and *Zodiac* (2007), shot the film almost entirely in a cold blue-grey palette, matching the visual aesthetic of the Facebook interface of the era. Principal photography took place from October 2009 to January 2010. Fincher shot on the Red One digital camera, choosing it for its ability to render dark scenes with fine detail — a critical requirement for the many interior scenes in dorm rooms, clubs, and server rooms that form the film's visual world. The opening sequence — a rapid-fire dialogue scene between Zuckerberg and his girlfriend Erica at a Boston bar — was reportedly shot more than 99 times to achieve the exact rhythm Fincher wanted.

The casting of Jesse Eisenberg was largely unknown to mainstream audiences at the time. Eisenberg's previous feature credits included *Zombieland* (2009) and *The Squid and the Whale* (2005), but he was not a major star. His ability to deliver Sorkin's high-speed technical monologues without losing the emotional undertow of the character was the central performance challenge; Eisenberg spent three months in preparation before filming began. Justin Timberlake — then still primarily known as a musician — was cast as Sean Parker after Fincher identified him as the right combination of charm and volatility for the role.

## Awards, Reception, and Legacy

*The Social Network* dominated the critics' organization awards season that preceded the Academy Awards. It won the National Board of Review's Best Film award, the American Film Institute's Film of the Year, the New York Film Critics Circle, the Los Angeles Film Critics Association, and the Broadcast Film Critics Association — among others. After the major critics' groups voted it best picture almost unanimously, the Oscar outcome seemed almost certain, making the eventual Best Picture loss to *The King's Speech* at the 83rd Academy Awards one of the most debated upsets in Oscar history.

The film won three Academy Awards: Best Adapted Screenplay (Aaron Sorkin), Best Original Score (Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross), and Best Film Editing (Kirk Baxter and Angus Wall). Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross's score — recorded using electronic instruments, prepared piano, and digital manipulation — became one of the most influential film scores of the decade, establishing a template for mood-based atmospheric scoring that was widely imitated. The duo won the Grammy Award for Best Score Soundtrack for Visual Media in 2012. The film additionally won four Golden Globe Awards, including Best Motion Picture — Drama and Best Director.

## Significance

*The Social Network* arrived at a moment when Facebook — founded in 2004 — had just passed 500 million registered users (announced July 2010), was becoming a global cultural and commercial force, and was preparing a widely anticipated public offering. Its release crystallized the mythology of Silicon Valley entrepreneurship in popular culture: the brilliant, socially awkward founder; the betrayed friend; the shortcuts taken in pursuit of world-changing scale. Aaron Sorkin's rapid-fire dialogue and David Fincher's cold, precise visual style were widely praised as fitting the story's themes of speed, obsession, and manipulation.

Zuckerberg himself stated in 2010 that he had not watched the film and that based on reports of its contents he felt it had "a lot of stuff that's just not true." The film's dramatic liberties — including the portrayal of the creation of Facemash and Zuckerberg's relationship with Saverin — have been extensively documented by journalists and participants. This fact-versus-narrative ambiguity became itself part of the film's cultural meaning: it was received as a parable about the tech industry rather than a literal biography, and its influence on public perception of Silicon Valley founders was arguably greater precisely because it was mythologized.

The film influenced subsequent biographical films about technology entrepreneurs — including *Jobs* (2013), *Steve Jobs* (2015), and *The Founder* (2016) — and is frequently cited as one of the defining films of the 2010s. The Trent Reznor / Atticus Ross score became highly influential in the genre of electronic and ambient film music.
