# Toy Story 3

**Category**: Arts & Culture

**Key figures**: Lee Unkrich (director), Michael Arndt (screenplay), Randy Newman (composer), Tom Hanks (voice of Woody), Tim Allen (voice of Buzz Lightyear), Joan Cusack (Jessie), Ned Beatty (Lotso), Michael Keaton (Ken)

## Summary

Toy Story 3, directed by Lee Unkrich and released on June 18, 2010, grossed $1.067 billion worldwide, establishing itself as the highest-grossing film of 2010 and the first animated feature ever to surpass $1 billion at the global box office. The film earned $415 million in domestic (U.S./Canada) release and $652 million internationally, surpassing its $200 million production budget more than fivefold. The narrative centers on Woody, Buzz, and the toy ensemble confronting obsolescence and separation as their owner Andy prepares to leave for college, blending humor, emotional depth, and philosophical reflection on mortality and legacy.

At the 83rd Academy Awards (held February 27, 2011), Toy Story 3 won **Best Animated Feature** and **Best Original Song** (Randy Newman's "We Belong Together"), while earning nominations for Best Picture, Best Adapted Screenplay, and Best Sound Editing. It became only the third animated film ever nominated for Best Picture — following Beauty and the Beast (1991) and Up (2009) — and was the first since Beauty and the Beast to earn the nomination outside the Animated Feature category introduced in 2002. The film achieved a **98% approval rating** on Rotten Tomatoes based on 329 reviews and a Metacritic score of 92/100. It remained the highest-grossing animated film of all time until Disney's Frozen surpassed it in January 2014.

## Production and Development

Pixar began development on Toy Story 3 in 2006, following Disney's $7.4 billion acquisition of Pixar in January of that year. Lee Unkrich, who had co-directed Toy Story 2 (1999) and Finding Nemo (2003), was promoted to solo director. Screenwriter Michael Arndt — who had won the Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay for Little Miss Sunshine (2006) — crafted the central story arc of Andy departing for Dartmouth College and inadvertently donating the toys to Sunnyside Daycare, where the seemingly benevolent teddy bear Lotso (voiced by Ned Beatty) reveals himself as a dictatorial villain.

Production took approximately four years, with Pixar employing roughly 600 artists. The film introduced several new characters, including Ken (Michael Keaton), Barbie's counterpart, whose comedic fashion sequences drew on Mattel's cooperation. John Lasseter, Pixar's chief creative officer, served as executive producer and helped shape the emotional finale in which Andy gives his toys to young neighbor Bonnie before departing — a scene widely cited by critics and audiences as among the most emotionally resonant in Pixar's history.

## Box Office and Commercial Performance

Toy Story 3 opened to $110.3 million in its opening weekend (June 18–20, 2010), the second-largest opening weekend for an animated film at the time, behind only Shrek the Third's $121.6 million debut in 2007. Internationally, the film performed particularly strongly in China, Japan, and the United Kingdom, where it grossed £57 million. It crossed $600 million globally within three weeks of release.

The film's financial success was amplified by significant merchandise and licensing revenue. Retail sales of Toy Story 3 products exceeded $2 billion worldwide in 2010, with Mattel's Lotso plush bear becoming one of the year's best-selling toys despite the character's villainous role in the film. Overall franchise merchandise revenue from Toy Story exceeded $3 billion by year's end.

## Critical Reception

Critics emphasized the film's unexpected emotional depth alongside its technical achievement. Roger Ebert awarded it four stars, writing that it demonstrated "how an animated film can transcend its origins and speak directly to the human heart." A.O. Scott of The New York Times called it "a work of total mastery" that "deepens our attachment to the characters." In annual critics' polls, Toy Story 3 ranked first on numerous "best films of 2010" lists.

The film's Dante-esque incinerator sequence — in which the toys appear to face incineration before a last-second rescue by the Alien squeeze toys — generated particular critical discussion as an unusually dark passage for a family film. Pixar's animation team worked on the sequence for eight months, creating realistic fire simulations using proprietary rendering software.

## Academy Awards and Industry Recognition

Beyond its Oscar wins, Toy Story 3 received recognition from virtually every major critics' organization in North America:

- **Golden Globe Award for Best Animated Feature Film** (January 16, 2011)
- **BAFTA Award for Best Animated Film** (February 13, 2011)
- **Annie Award for Best Animated Feature** (February 5, 2011)
- **National Board of Review**: Best Animated Film of 2010
- **American Film Institute**: Top 10 Films of 2010

Its Best Picture Oscar nomination — one of ten in the expanded field introduced in 2010 — reflected the Academy's recognition that animated films could compete on equal terms with live-action prestige productions.

## Significance

Toy Story 3's unprecedented box-office scale and critical acclaim crystallized mainstream acceptance of computer animation and elevated animated films from children's entertainment to prestige Oscar contenders. The film's success demonstrated Pixar's creative and commercial dominance in the post-2006 Disney-Pixar integration, justifying Disney's acquisition. Its emotional maturity — exploring aging, friendship, mortality, and the passage of time through the lens of childhood treasures — resonated across generational audiences who had grown up with Woody and Buzz since 1995.

The film's commercial performance catalyzed a wave of animated blockbusters and franchise sequels throughout the 2010s. Toy Story 3 marked the culmination of a 15-year arc from Toy Story's 1995 revolutionary introduction of CGI feature animation to mainstream acceptance of the medium as a primary storytelling vehicle, rivaling live-action cinema in both artistry and economic return.

A fourth installment, Toy Story 4, was released in June 2019 and also won the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature, extending the franchise's Oscar record.
