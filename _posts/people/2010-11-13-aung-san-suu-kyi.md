## Summary

Aung San Suu Kyi, the pro-democracy leader of Myanmar's National League for Democracy (NLD), was released from house arrest on November 13, 2010, after spending a cumulative total of approximately 15 years in detention across three separate periods spanning more than two decades. Born June 19, 1945, in Rangoon, she was the daughter of General Aung San, the hero of Burmese independence who was assassinated on July 19, 1947, when she was two years old. After studying at the University of Delhi and Oxford University (where she earned a BA in Philosophy, Politics, and Economics from St Hugh's College in 1967), she worked at the United Nations in New York before returning to Burma in April 1988 to care for her ailing mother — an act that propelled her to the center of the country's democratic uprising.

Her November 2010 release came just six days after Myanmar's military government held multiparty elections on November 7 — elections widely condemned by international observers as fraudulent, which produced a predictable victory for the military-aligned Union Solidarity and Development Party (USDP). That the junta released her immediately after securing its electoral outcome made the timing transparent: her imprisonment had become an international liability, and her freedom was a managed concession intended to signal a controlled opening rather than genuine democratic reform. Nonetheless, her release was a watershed moment in Myanmar's modern history and a symbol of resilience that drew global attention.

## Three Periods of Detention

Aung San Suu Kyi's detention was not continuous but fell into three distinct periods under the State Protection Law (1975), which allowed detention without charge for up to five years, renewable indefinitely:

**First detention (July 20, 1989 – July 10, 1995):** She was placed under house arrest at her family home at 54 University Avenue, Rangoon, approximately one year after she had returned to Burma and become the NLD's general secretary. In May 1990, the military held general elections that the NLD won by a landslide — 392 of 485 parliamentary seats, with 59.9 percent of the vote. The junta (then called SLORC) voided the results, refused to convene parliament, and kept her confined. She was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in October 1991 while still under detention; her sons Alexander and Kim accepted the prize on her behalf in Oslo, and she donated the $1.3 million prize money to a health and education trust fund for the Burmese people.

**Second detention (September 23, 2000 – May 6, 2002):** After a period of conditional freedom during which she was blocked from travelling outside Rangoon, she was re-detained following an NLD march she attempted to lead toward Mandalay.

**Third detention (May 30, 2003 – November 13, 2010):** The longest and most internationally scrutinized period followed the Depayin massacre, in which government-organized militias attacked her motorcade in northern Burma, killing an estimated 70 NLD supporters and supporters who had gathered to see her. She was taken into "protective custody" and remained confined for more than seven years. This detention was renewed in 2006, 2008, and finally 2009 — when the junta added 18 months for an incident in which an American, John Yettaw, swam uninvited to her lakeside home, giving the government a pretext to extend her confinement through the 2010 elections.

## Political Context of the 2010 Release

The November 7, 2010 elections were Myanmar's first multiparty elections in 20 years, conducted under a military-drafted 2008 constitution that reserved 25 percent of parliamentary seats for the military and required the commander-in-chief's approval for constitutional amendments. The NLD boycotted the elections, judging them neither free nor fair. The USDP's official "victory" — with roughly 80 percent of the vote under conditions that international monitors were barred from fully observing — was therefore unsurprising.

Six days later, on November 13, 2010, the gates of her University Avenue home opened and Aung San Suu Kyi walked free. The scene — thousands of supporters crowding the street outside, her waving from the gate — was broadcast around the world and produced an outpouring of international relief. Her husband, British academic Michael Aris, had died of prostate cancer in Oxford in March 1999 without being allowed to visit her in her final years of detention; she had refused to leave Burma to be with him in his final illness, knowing she would not be permitted to return. His death was among the personal costs the junta extracted as part of her detention.

## Post-Release Political Career

Following her 2010 release, Aung San Suu Kyi re-engaged in national political life at a pace that surprised observers:

- **April 1, 2012**: In parliamentary by-elections, she won the Kawhmu constituency seat, entering parliament for the first time at age 66.
- **November 8, 2015**: The NLD won a supermajority in general elections — 390 of 440 contested lower-house seats, approximately 77 percent of elected seats — in Myanmar's most credible elections in decades.
- **March 30, 2016**: Became State Counsellor of Myanmar, the de facto head of government. Myanmar's 2008 constitution barred anyone with a foreign spouse or children from the presidency (a provision written specifically to exclude her), so the State Counsellor role was created to reflect her actual authority.
- **2016–2017**: The Rohingya crisis escalated dramatically, culminating in military operations in Rakhine State that the UN and others characterized as ethnic cleansing and possibly genocide. Her failure to condemn the military or protect the Rohingya drew intense international criticism, with multiple honorary degrees and awards rescinded, including Amnesty International's Ambassador of Conscience Award (2018).
- **February 1, 2021**: The Myanmar military launched a coup, arresting Aung San Suu Kyi and President Win Myint. Military courts subsequently sentenced her to a total of 27 years in prison across multiple trials conducted in secret.

## Significance

Aung San Suu Kyi's 2010 release was a pivotal moment in Myanmar's modern history. For over two decades her imprisonment had galvanized international attention, earned her the Nobel Peace Prize (1991), and kept democratic aspirations alive in a country where dissent was violently suppressed. The prize, given "for her non-violent struggle for democracy and human rights," placed her in the tradition of Nelson Mandela, whose 27-year imprisonment had similarly become the focal point of global anti-apartheid solidarity.

Her release in November 2010 marked the symbolic end of Myanmar's darkest era of strict isolation and the beginning of a contested transition — a transition that initially produced genuine democratic advances before the 2021 coup returned the country to military rule. Her legacy became profoundly complicated by the Rohingya crisis and her failure to use her moral authority to defend a persecuted minority, events that shadowed and qualified the moral clarity of her earlier resistance. The arc from Shwedagon Pagoda speech (1988) to Nobel Prize (1991) to State Counsellor (2016) to imprisonment (2021) traces one of the most complex political trajectories in post-Cold War democracy movements.
