## Summary

During the [2010 FIFA World Cup in South Africa]({{ '/news/history-politics/fifa-world-cup-2010/' | relative_url }}), the vuvuzela — a roughly 65-centimeter plastic horn — became one of the tournament's most distinctive and controversial features. The instrument produces a single sustained tone near B-flat at approximately 127 decibels at close range, comparable to the volume of a chainsaw or a jet engine at 100 metres. Stadiums packed with tens of thousands of fans blowing simultaneously created a continuous wall of sound that defined the acoustic character of every match.

The vuvuzela has roots in southern African culture stretching back decades, derived from animal-horn instruments traditionally used in Swazi ceremonies, though the modern plastic form became popularised in South African football culture in the 1990s. Neil van Schalkwyk, a South African entrepreneur, filed a trademark for the plastic version in the early 2000s and supplied large quantities to World Cup events. By the time South Africa was awarded the 2010 tournament, the vuvuzela had become inseparable from the atmosphere at domestic football fixtures.

## International Reaction and the Ban Debate

The instrument divided global audiences almost immediately when the tournament opened on June 11, 2010. Television networks in dozens of countries received complaints that the vuvuzela's constant drone drowned out commentary, crowd atmosphere, and audible team communication on the pitch. The BBC fielded thousands of viewer complaints. Several European broadcasters experimented with audio filters and sound-mixing adjustments to separate the vuvuzela from other audio frequencies, with limited success.

FIFA received formal requests from national football associations — including Germany, France, and Australia — to ban the instrument from stadiums. On June 14, 2010, FIFA president Sepp Blatter publicly ruled against a ban, stating that "vuvuzelas are part of South African football culture" and that silencing them would deprive the host nation of its authentic identity. FIFA later confirmed that the instruments would remain permitted throughout the tournament.

## Cultural and Commercial Dimensions

South African fans and cultural commentators strongly rejected the call for a ban, arguing that it reflected a colonial imposition of European sporting aesthetics onto African celebration traditions. The controversy prompted substantial coverage about whose cultural norms should govern a global event held on African soil for the first time — South Africa's was the first African host of the men's World Cup.

The international attention generated enormous commercial interest. Toy manufacturers and sporting goods retailers worldwide rushed to produce and stock vuvuzelas, and reports indicated that millions of plastic versions were exported during the tournament window. The sound became embedded in global popular culture: YouTube videos of the vuvuzela's tone gained tens of millions of views; applications that simulated the sound were among the most downloaded in app stores during June–July 2010. Remixes of the sound appeared in electronic music and advertising.

## Health Concerns

Audiologists and public health researchers raised concerns during the tournament about hearing damage from prolonged exposure. The South African Medical Journal published observations that vuvuzela noise at stadium levels — recorded between 113 and 131 dB depending on distance — exceeded safe thresholds under sustained use, and that fans who attended multiple matches faced real risk of noise-induced hearing loss. Hearing protection was sold at stadium entrances throughout the tournament.

## Legacy and Later Restrictions

The 2010 World Cup normalised debate about instruments and ambient sound at football events. After 2010, FIFA and the Confederation of African Football tightened regulations on vuvuzelas at subsequent tournaments, ultimately restricting them from many international venues citing spectator experience. The instrument remained permitted in South African domestic football. The episode became a widely cited case study in sports media studies regarding cultural difference, broadcast standards, and the politics of representation in global sport.
