# 2010 Russian Heat Wave and Wildfires

**Category**: History & Politics  
**Key figures**: Prime Minister Vladimir Putin (Russia), President Dmitry Medvedev (Russia), Emergency Situations Minister Sergei Shoigu, Alexander Ginzburg and colleagues (Berger Institute of General Pathology, mortality research), Randall Dole and colleagues (NOAA, attribution research)

## Summary

The summer of 2010 saw an unprecedented heat wave grip western Russia, with temperatures reaching 44°C (111°F) — a national record — accompanied by hundreds of wildfires that burned across European Russia from late July through early September. The extreme conditions triggered severe air pollution in Moscow and other cities, creating a public health catastrophe. Combined with the direct fire deaths and heat-related mortality, the event claimed tens of thousands of lives and resulted in approximately $15 billion in economic damages, making it one of the most severe natural disasters in modern Russian history.

The heat wave covered an area of approximately 2 million square kilometers — roughly 50 times the size of Switzerland — with temperature anomalies between 6.7°C and 13.3°C above normal. The wildfires burned over 300,000 hectares primarily in western Russia, with smoke and ash creating hazardous air quality across a vast region. Russia had not recorded such extremes in its 130-year instrumental record, and paleoclimate analyses suggest the 2010 summer temperatures exceeded anything in western Russia for at least 500 years.

## Meteorological Background

The heat wave was driven by an exceptionally persistent blocking anticyclone—a high-pressure system—that stalled over western Russia from mid-July through late August. Normally the mid-latitude circulation moves such weather patterns along within days, but in 2010 the anticyclone became quasi-stationary, producing the longest sustained heat wave in Moscow's recorded history: the city recorded 34 consecutive days with temperatures above 30°C (86°F), from July 6 to August 8.

A significant debate emerged among climate scientists about attribution. In 2011, a NOAA research team led by Randall Dole concluded that the Russian heat wave was primarily due to natural atmospheric variability and could not be directly attributed to anthropogenic climate change. A competing analysis by Stefan Rahmstorf and Dim Coumou (Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research, 2011) argued that global warming had increased the probability of such extremes by a factor of approximately five. Subsequent attribution research tended to support Rahmstorf and Coumou's interpretation: climate change was shown to have loaded the dice toward such extremes, even if internal variability triggered the specific blocking event.

## The Wildfires

By late July, extremely dry conditions combined with the record heat to ignite widespread fires across European Russia. The fires had three main causes: negligent human behavior (unextinguished campfires, fireworks, and agricultural burning), the highly flammable conditions created by the drainage of vast peatlands during the Soviet era, and the extraordinary heat and wind.

The drained peatlands proved particularly consequential. From the 1960s through the 1980s, Soviet agricultural planners drained approximately 500,000 hectares of peat bogs in the Moscow region to create cropland. After Soviet-era drainage systems fell into disrepair following 1991, the dried peat accumulated as a combustible layer. When ignited, peat fires are notoriously difficult to extinguish—they can smolder underground for months—and they release large quantities of carbon dioxide and toxic smoke.

Prime Minister Vladimir Putin visited fire zones and centralized the coordination of emergency response. Emergency Situations Minister Sergei Shoigu oversaw deployments of tens of thousands of firefighters, military personnel, and volunteers. Russia also requested international assistance, receiving firefighting aircraft from France, Italy, and other countries. Despite these efforts, the fires were not fully extinguished until late September, following the return of more normal temperatures and rainfall.

## Public Health Crisis and Mortality

The human toll of the 2010 Russian heat wave was severe and multifaceted. Mortality increased through three mechanisms: direct heat-related deaths (hyperthermia, cardiovascular events), respiratory deaths from wildfire smoke and air pollution, and drowning deaths as Russians sought relief in bodies of water.

In Moscow, daily mortality approximately doubled from a baseline of around 360 deaths per day to over 700 deaths per day during the peak weeks of late July and early August. Alexander Ginzburg and colleagues at the Berger Institute of General Pathology estimated total excess mortality in Moscow from July–August 2010 at approximately 11,000 deaths.

Across Russia as a whole, estimates of total excess deaths ranged from approximately 55,000 (Centre for Research on the Epidemiology of Disasters) to 56,000 (Barriopedro et al., 2011, *Science* journal). The 2010 heat wave may have caused more deaths than any single natural disaster in Russian history.

Moscow's air quality deteriorated to hazardous levels as smoke from the surrounding fires blanketed the city. On August 7, the concentration of fine particulate matter (PM2.5) in Moscow reached approximately 700 micrograms per cubic meter—more than 28 times the WHO's recommended safe level of 25 μg/m³. Visibility in central Moscow fell to fewer than 10 meters. The Moscow government closed parks, banned outdoor sports events, and advised residents to stay indoors.

## Agricultural and Economic Impact

The heat wave devastated Russian agriculture. Drought conditions affected approximately 27 regions of European Russia, destroying an estimated 25–30% of the country's grain harvest. Russia had expected to produce 95 million tonnes of grain in 2010; the actual harvest was approximately 60 million tonnes—a shortfall of nearly 35 million tonnes.

In response, Russia imposed a total grain export ban from August 15, 2010, through June 2011, the first such ban since the Soviet era. Russia, normally one of the world's top wheat exporters, supplied approximately 10% of global wheat exports; the sudden withdrawal contributed to a spike in global wheat prices, which rose approximately 60–70% during August–September 2010. Food security analysts cited the export ban as a contributor to food-price pressures that fueled discontent across North Africa and the Middle East in the months that followed.

The Russian government estimated total economic damages at approximately 1.25 trillion rubles, equivalent to about $40 billion at 2010 exchange rates, though widely cited estimates range from $10–15 billion depending on whether indirect losses are included.

## Policy Response

The 2010 disaster prompted significant policy changes in Russia. President Medvedev, initially dismissive of climate science, publicly acknowledged after the fires that "What's happening with the world's climate right now needs to be a wake-up call to all of us." Russia subsequently joined the Copenhagen Accord's list of countries making voluntary emissions pledges, though it did not ratify binding commitments at the time.

Domestically, the disaster prompted a major reform of Russia's forest management system. The 2007 Forest Code, which had decentralized fire-management responsibilities to regional governments and commercial forest leaseholders, was criticized for contributing to inadequate suppression capacity. Following 2010, the federal government restored greater central oversight and increased firefighting resources.

The disaster also accelerated efforts to re-wet formerly drained peatlands around Moscow. The Moscow Peatland Rewetting Project, supported by environmental groups and the Moscow city government, worked to restore water levels in key peatlands to reduce the risk of future peat fires.

## Significance

The 2010 Russian heat wave and wildfires represented an unprecedented climate extreme for the region in the modern instrumental record (dating back to 1880). The event had profound implications for understanding climate change and extreme weather: Europe had not experienced such large summer temperature anomalies in the previous 500 years. The combination of temperature records, widespread wildfires, and massive mortality highlighted the vulnerability of modern societies to compound natural disasters and environmental hazards.

The disaster prompted global scientific discussion about the connection between climate change and extreme weather events, and elevated awareness of Russia's susceptibility to climate-driven crises. It also served as a dramatic illustration of how land-use decisions made decades earlier—Soviet-era peatland drainage—can amplify future climate impacts.

The 2010 Russian disaster was part of a wider pattern of climate extremes that year: 2010 set or tied numerous records globally, with the year ultimately ranking as one of the two warmest years on record at the time (tied with 2005). The same period saw record flooding in [Pakistan](pakistan-floods.md), record snowfall in the United States, and severe drought in several other regions, prompting climate scientists to characterize 2010 as a year of "climate extremes."

## Key Facts

- **Temperature Record**: The national high of 44°C (111°F) was recorded on July 11, 2010, in Yashkul, Kalmykia, surpassing the previous record set in 1940; Moscow recorded 34 consecutive days above 30°C (July 6–August 8), the longest such streak in 130 years of records.
- **Geographic Extent**: The heat wave covered approximately 2 million km²; 300,000+ hectares burned across 19 regions.
- **Duration**: Mid-July through early September 2010, with peak intensity from July 26 to August 8.
- **Total Excess Deaths**: Estimated 55,000–56,000 excess deaths nationally; approximately 11,000 excess deaths in Moscow alone.
- **Moscow Mortality Peak**: Daily deaths in Moscow reached approximately 700 — roughly double the baseline — during the peak weeks in late July and early August.
- **Worst Air Quality**: Moscow's PM2.5 particulate concentration reached ~700 μg/m³ on August 7, 2010 — 28 times WHO safe levels.
- **Direct Wildfire Deaths**: Approximately 54 direct fatalities from fires themselves; hundreds more from heat exposure and drowning.
- **Grain Harvest Shortfall**: Harvest of approximately 60 million tonnes vs. expected 95 million tonnes — a shortfall of ~35 million tonnes.
- **Export Ban**: Russia banned grain exports August 15, 2010–June 2011, contributing to a 60–70% spike in global wheat prices.
- **Economic Damages**: Estimated at approximately $15 billion (to $40 billion including indirect losses).
- **Peatland Factor**: Soviet-era drainage of ~500,000 hectares of peat bogs created highly combustible conditions; peat fires are nearly impossible to extinguish once established.
- **Climate Attribution**: Rahmstorf and Coumou (2011) estimated climate change had increased the probability of such extreme heat by approximately fivefold; NOAA contested the attribution (Dole et al., 2011).
- **Regional Emergency**: Nineteen of Russia's 83 regions declared states of emergency following crop failure and environmental damage.
- **Policy Consequence**: Accelerated Russia's peatland rewetting programs and prompted reforms to the 2007 Forest Code's decentralized firefighting structure.
