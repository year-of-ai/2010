# 2010 Thai Political Crisis

**Category**: History & Politics  
**Key figures**: Thaksin Shinawatra (ousted former PM, in exile), Abhisit Vejjajiva (Prime Minister), Suthep Thaugsuban (Deputy PM), Anupong Paojinda (Army Chief), Khattiya Sawasdipol ("Seh Daeng," renegade general), United Front for Democracy Against Dictatorship (UDD / Red Shirts)

## Summary

Thailand's pro-democracy Red Shirt movement—the United Front for Democracy Against Dictatorship (UDD), representing rural and working-class interests aligned with ousted former Prime Minister Thaksin Shinawatra—occupied Bangkok's central Ratchaprasong intersection and surrounding luxury business district beginning March 14, 2010, demanding the dissolution of parliament and new elections. The protest grew to an estimated 90,000 demonstrators at its peak, the largest single political mobilization in Thai history in decades. After two months of occupation that paralyzed the commercial heart of Bangkok, on May 19, 2010, the Thai military launched a final crackdown, deploying armored vehicles and firing live ammunition into protest areas. The subsequent urban fighting killed at least 91 people—including civilians, soldiers, medical workers, and journalists—and wounded over 1,800. Thailand was left under martial law in key provinces and deeply politically polarized, heading toward elections in July 2011.

## Background

The crisis had roots in the September 2006 military coup that deposed Prime Minister Thaksin Shinawatra while he was abroad, citing corruption allegations and abuse of power. Subsequent courts dissolved Thaksin's Thai Rak Thai party and eventually banned him from politics for two years. The "Yellow Shirt" movement—the People's Alliance for Democracy (PAD), backed by urban middle-class royalists and anti-Thaksin constituencies—had itself occupied Bangkok's Suvarnabhumi and Don Mueang international airports in November 2008, forcing the collapse of two Thaksin-aligned governments in succession. The ensuing parliamentary reshuffling installed Abhisit Vejjajiva's Democrat Party government without a general election, which Red Shirt protesters characterized as an illegitimate elite-engineered outcome. At the time of the 2010 crisis, Thaksin lived in self-imposed exile in Dubai, coordinating with Red Shirt leaders and addressing protests via live video link, demonstrating how modern communications technology transformed the dynamics of exile politics.

## Timeline of Events

- **March 14, 2010**: Red Shirt protesters begin occupying the Ratchaprasong intersection—home to Bangkok's premium shopping malls including Central World, Siam Paragon, and Gaysorn Plaza—blocking commercial traffic in the capital's economic core.
- **April 7, 2010**: The government declares a state of emergency in Bangkok and five surrounding provinces, granting security forces broader powers to act against protesters.
- **April 10, 2010**: First major violent confrontations. Government troops attempt to disperse protesters at the Phan Fa Bridge area; street battles erupt across central Bangkok. The clashes kill 25 people—21 civilians and 4 soldiers—and injure over 800, marking the worst political violence in Thailand since May 1992.
- **April 22–May 12, 2010**: Multiple rounds of negotiations between Abhisit's government and UDD leaders fail. Abhisit offers a November 2010 election date; the UDD initially accepts, then withdraws acceptance under internal pressure.
- **May 13, 2010**: Renegade General Khattiya Sawasdipol (widely known as "Seh Daeng"), a serving Thai Army general who had publicly backed the protesters and organized armed "Black Shirt" elements within the encampment, is shot in the head by a sniper while giving an interview to a *New York Times* correspondent. He dies on May 17, becoming the highest-ranking fatality of the crisis.
- **May 19, 2010**: The military launches a final crackdown operation from multiple directions simultaneously, using armored personnel carriers, snipers, and live fire to compress the protest perimeter at Ratchaprasong. By evening, the UDD's core leadership surrenders and turns themselves in to police. Scattered protesters respond by setting fire to more than two dozen buildings, including the Central World shopping complex—one of the largest malls in Southeast Asia—and the stock exchange building, causing hundreds of millions of dollars in additional damage.
- **May 23, 2010**: Government declares restoration of order; martial law remains in effect in 24 provinces.

## Significance

The 2010 Thai political crisis produced the deadliest urban street confrontation in modern Thai history since the October 1973 and May 1992 uprisings, and represented a decisive acceleration of the post-2006 political conflict between populist and establishment forces. The military's deployment of lethal force against civilian protesters—and the political system's failure to negotiate a peaceful settlement—deepened distrust of Thai democratic institutions on all sides and set the stage for the 2014 coup that would again topple a Thaksin-aligned government following subsequent protests.

The deaths of journalists, a medical volunteer, and foreign correspondents during the crackdown drew international condemnation. Like contemporaneous democratic struggles—including [the Tunisian Jasmine Revolution](arab-spring-tunisia.md) months later, which triggered the broader Arab Spring—the Thai conflict reflected global tensions between entrenched power structures and popular movements demanding democratic accountability. The episode also demonstrated the vulnerability of democratic transitions in Southeast Asia, a concern that resonated alongside simultaneous developments such as the continued house arrest of Myanmar's [Aung San Suu Kyi](../people/aung-san-suu-kyi.md). Leaked US diplomatic cables subsequently published by [WikiLeaks](wikileaks-cables.md) in late 2010 included detailed US Embassy Bangkok assessments of the Royal Thai Army's strategic calculations and the monarchy's behind-the-scenes influence on the crisis, adding further controversy to an already deeply contested episode.
