# 2010 Haiti Earthquake

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: René Préval (President of Haiti), Ban Ki-moon (UN Secretary-General), Hillary Clinton (US Secretary of State)

## Summary

On January 12, 2010, at 16:53 local time, a magnitude 7.0 earthquake struck Haiti approximately 25 kilometers west of the capital Port-au-Prince, near the town of Léogâne. The quake occurred at a shallow depth of 13 kilometers on the Enriquillo-Plantain Garden fault system and lasted less than 30 seconds. Its shallow depth and proximity to Port-au-Prince, a densely populated metropolitan area with approximately 3 million inhabitants and widespread informal construction, combined to produce catastrophic destruction.

The official Haitian government death toll ranged from 220,000 to 316,000, though independent academic studies estimated between 137,000 and 160,000 deaths; all figures reflect the extraordinary difficulty of counting casualties amid collapsed infrastructure. Approximately 300,000 people were injured and 1.5 million were displaced, with many living in spontaneous outdoor settlements for months following the event. The earthquake severely damaged or destroyed an estimated 250,000 residences and 30,000 commercial buildings. Key national institutions including the National Palace, Port-au-Prince Cathedral, the National Assembly building, and numerous hospitals were destroyed or rendered uninhabitable. Total economic losses were estimated at $7.8 to $8.5 billion — equivalent to more than 120 percent of Haiti's 2009 gross domestic product.

The international humanitarian response was the largest multinational mobilization to a single disaster to that point. The United States deployed 17 Navy ships, 48 helicopters, 12 fixed-wing aircraft, and approximately 10,000 military personnel. Over 20 countries committed military assets within days. The American Red Cross raised $50 million in the first weeks, with $32.5 million raised through text-message donations alone. The United Nations significantly expanded its peacekeeping mission (MINUSTAH) and coordinated relief operations across dozens of aid organizations.

## Immediate Aftermath and Infrastructure Collapse

The physical destruction was concentrated in Port-au-Prince and the nearby municipalities of Léogâne (the town closest to the epicenter, where roughly 90 percent of structures were destroyed), Jacmel, and Carrefour. The earthquake occurred during afternoon business hours, when schools, government offices, markets, and workplaces were fully occupied — maximizing casualties in buildings that were structurally unsuited to survive even moderate ground motion. Haiti's building stock was characterized by widespread use of unreinforced concrete block construction with poor mortar quality, unenforced setback requirements, and minimal rebar, all compounded by decades of deforestation that had increased slope instability and liquefaction risk.

The destruction of Haiti's state infrastructure was nearly total. The National Palace — the colonial-era seat of government — suffered partial collapse of its central dome. The Ministry of Finance, the Ministry of Public Works, and the justice palace were destroyed. The United Nations Stabilization Mission in Haiti (MINUSTAH) compound at the Christopher Hotel collapsed, killing 101 UN staff members including the Mission's chief, Hédi Annabi — the single deadliest day for the United Nations in its history. Three Haitian Supreme Court justices died. The main port in Port-au-Prince sustained severe damage, complicating the delivery of relief supplies by sea during the critical first days. Port-au-Prince's Toussaint Louverture International Airport, though structurally intact, was overwhelmed by the volume of relief flights within 24 hours of the earthquake, requiring U.S. military air traffic controllers to assume management to prevent gridlock.

## International Response and Aid Coordination

The scale of international mobilization was unprecedented. Within 72 hours, U.S. President Barack Obama authorized the deployment of the 82nd Airborne Division and USS Carl Vinson carrier strike group; the USS Carl Vinson arrived January 14 and served as an offshore command and logistics platform throughout the response. The U.S. Agency for International Development (USAID) activated Urban Search and Rescue teams from Los Angeles, Fairfax County, and Miami-Dade within hours of the earthquake. Canada, France, the Dominican Republic, and Cuba were among the first countries to deploy field hospitals and medical personnel.

The mobilization surfaced serious coordination failures. More than 120 countries and over 1,000 non-governmental organizations operated in Haiti within weeks of the earthquake, with no unified command structure beyond a UN cluster system that many organizations bypassed. The centralized processing of aid at the airport and port created severe bottlenecks: on several days in the first week, aircraft carrying relief supplies were diverted or turned away because the airport had reached capacity. Food and water distribution reached many neighborhoods too slowly; in some areas, desperate survivors organized impromptu settlements on open ground before any official aid arrived.

The Inter-American Development Bank, World Bank, and International Monetary Fund announced debt cancellation and emergency credit facilities totaling over $1 billion in January 2010. A donor conference at the United Nations in March 2010 pledged a further $5.3 billion in reconstruction aid for a two-year period, with total commitments reaching approximately $9 billion over ten years. Independent assessments subsequently found that disbursement, accountability, and housing reconstruction consistently fell short of pledged targets.

## The 2010 Cholera Outbreak

Beginning in mid-October 2010, a cholera epidemic of extraordinary severity swept through Haiti — the first outbreak of cholera in the country in nearly a century. The Vibrio cholerae bacterium was introduced into the Artibonite River, Haiti's largest waterway and a primary source of drinking water, by effluent from a MINUSTAH base occupied by a Nepali battalion. By the end of 2010, more than 120,000 cases and over 2,500 deaths had been recorded; ultimately the outbreak killed an estimated 9,800 to 10,000 Haitians and infected approximately 820,000 people.

The United Nations initially denied responsibility, commissioning an independent investigation (the "Haitian Panel of Experts") whose 2011 report concluded the outbreak was most likely introduced from a contaminated source at the Mirebalais MINUSTAH base but did not attribute explicit fault. A subsequent independent scientific investigation by UN Special Rapporteur Philip Alston (2016) concluded unambiguously that the UN was responsible. In 2016, the UN Secretary-General formally apologized to Haiti and acknowledged the organization's role; however, the UN's immunity from civil litigation shielded it from court-ordered compensation, and a proposed $400 million victim support fund never received contributions sufficient to be operational.

The cholera epidemic compounded the earthquake's humanitarian legacy: it struck a population already weakened, displaced, and living in emergency conditions, and it demonstrated how post-disaster crowding and destroyed sanitation infrastructure could create cascading secondary catastrophes.

## Significance

The 2010 Haiti earthquake exposed the intersection of natural hazards and structural vulnerability produced by decades of poverty, political instability, inadequate building codes, and limited state capacity. Haiti's extreme losses — compared with the relatively lower casualty toll from the magnitude 8.8 Chile earthquake 46 days later, which killed approximately 525 people — became the most cited contemporary case study in how poverty and governance failures amplify natural disaster mortality.

The disaster generated the largest outpouring of charitable donations in history up to that point and drew global attention to Haiti's situation. However, subsequent assessments found significant gaps in aid coordination, reconstruction accountability, and long-term recovery outcomes. As of the 2020s, displacement and housing insecurity initiated by the earthquake had still not been fully resolved — estimates in the early 2020s placed the number of people still living in precarious earthquake-related displacement at tens of thousands.

The earthquake stands as one of the deadliest natural disasters of the twenty-first century and remains a central reference point in debates about humanitarian intervention, international development, and the relationship between poverty and disaster mortality. Its legacy is inextricable from the cholera crisis that followed, and from the gap between global pledges of assistance and delivered reconstruction.
